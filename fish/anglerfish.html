Anglerfish

Found all over the world, anglerfish species tend to be extremely sexually dimorphic, meaning that male anglerfish are very different from female anglerfish. In many anglerfish species, females are much larger than males, with a luminescent organ known as an esca at the top of their heads. This glowing organ lures prey towards the carnivorous fish and attracts males to facilitate mating. When a male finds a female to mate with, the small fish bites into the female's skin and releases an enzyme that breaks down the skin of both her body and his mouth, essentially fusing the two fish together. The male and female fish now share a circulatory system, and the male depends on the female for nutrients. In return, the female is now able to produce offspring. This fusion lasts for the rest of their lives. It is common for multiple males to be attached to a single female, although some taxa are monogamous.
